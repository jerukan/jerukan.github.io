Spatiotemporal Flux Probing for Single-Photon Videography

ECCV 2026 Submission (Paper ID #701)

We demonstrate videography via spatiotemporal flux probing across four sensor modalities: quanta image sensors, single-pixel asynchronous SPAD sensors, spike cameras, and event cameras. This webpage provides supplementary videos and qualitative comparisons accompanying the main results of the paper.

High-speed videography under extreme photon sparsity (Figure 1)

We captured a scene in which two foam bullets are fired at a balloon, using the SPAD512 camera. The scene is illuminated by ceiling lights flickering at 120 Hz and a faint 31 kHz LED. Our method reconstructs videos of the scene at various frame rates, revealing both phenomena and successfully recovers the high-speed trajectories as well as the balloon rupture. The 31 kHz flicker is not visible in the compressed video; we refer the reader to the supplementary file, where the flickering region is annotated with a red ellipse. We also simulate lower light levels by thinning the original photon detections.

Velocity-Selective Videography (Figure 1)

Our method automatically detects the velocity of linear motion in the scene and generates 100 kfps videos focused on the selected velocity. We demonstrate this on four objects with different motion types (diagonal, quasi-linear, horizontal, and vertical) and speeds, ranging from the slow-moving ECCV board to the fast-moving bullets. Each panel shows the recovered video from the velocity-selective reconstruction, where the target object appears sharp while all other content is suppressed.



Additional experiment: balloon popping with 20 kHz LED

We captured an additional scene with the SPAD512 camera, similar to Figure 1, but with a 20 kHz LED directed at the balloon. Our method reconstructs the scene at 100 kfps and successfully recovers the LED flicker while preserving finer spatial detail than bit2bit, which suppresses the 20 kHz signal, treating it as noise.



Qualitative Comparison with Existing Methods on the Blender scene (Figure 3, row 1)

We captured a scene with the SPAD512 showing a blender mixing lasagna and water being poured into a glass, under 120 Hz AC illumination flicker. We reconstruct 100 kfps video (displayed with a 50-frame skip) and compare against other methods. Only our method recovers both the global illumination flicker and the high-speed motion of the blender and pouring water.



Comparison with bit2bit (Figure 3, row 3)

We compare against bit2bit on the Mandrill scene from the bit2bit dataset, where our method successfully recovers the flicker of the CPU. We also captured a scene with the SPAD512 in which a static balloon is illuminated by a 31 kHz LED. Our method reconstructs the scene at 100 kfps and recovers the flicker, whereas bit2bit does not.

Comparison with Quanta Burst Photography (QBP) (Figure 3, row 3)

We compare against QBP on the guitar dataset. Our method successfully reconstructs the guitar strings even at 20x lower photon counts. QBP cannot recover the guitar strings at the lower light level, since they are not enough photons to accumulate.

Comparison with UWB (Figure 3, row 4)

We compare against UWB, which estimates temporal flux variations independently at each pixel. Even in this highly periodic scene, as photons become sparser the per-pixel estimates become increasingly noisy and UWB fails to recover the fan motion. Our method succeeds even at 10x lower photon counts, owing to its spatiotemporal aggregation across all pixels.

Ultra-Wideband Videography (Figure 3, row 5)

Our method also applies to single-pixel SPAD sensors, recovering ultra-wideband videos with GHz bandwidth. Compared to UWB, it produces higher-quality reconstructions, and its performance remains consistent even at 30x lower photon counts.

Simulated Results

We compare against bit2bit and UWB on several simulated scenes featuring diverse motions and phenomena. As the light level decreases, reconstruction quality degrades for all methods, but our method degrades more gracefully. Quantitative metrics are provided in the supplemental document.

Spike Camera Videography (Figure 4)

We extend our framework to spike cameras and demonstrate high-quality high-speed video reconstruction (2k fps for the car scene, 5 kfps for the rotation scene, and 10 kfps for the balloon scene), achieving quality comparable to state-of-the-art supervised deep learning methods.

Event Camera Videography (Figure 4)

We extend our framework to event cameras and demonstrate video reconstruction comparable to state-of-the-art Kalman filtering methods.
